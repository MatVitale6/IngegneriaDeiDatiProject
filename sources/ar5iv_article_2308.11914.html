Towards CausalGPT: A Multi-Agent Approach for Faithful Knowledge Reasoning via Promoting Causal Consistency in LLMs

Ziyi Tang 1 , Ruilin Wang 1 , Weixing Chen 1 , Keze Wang 1 , Yang Liu 1 , Tianshui Chen 2 , Liang Lin 1
Abstract

Despite advancements in LLMs, knowledge-based reasoning remains a longstanding issue due to the fragility of knowledge recall and inference. Existing methods primarily encourage LLMs to autonomously plan and solve problems or to extensively sample reasoning chains without addressing the conceptual and inferential fallacies. Attempting to alleviate inferential fallacies and drawing inspiration from multi-agent collaboration, we present a framework to increase faithfulness and causality for knowledge-based reasoning. Specifically, we propose to employ multiple intelligent agents (i.e., reasoners and an evaluator ) to work collaboratively in a reasoning-and-consensus paradigm for elevated reasoning faithfulness. The reasoners focus on providing solutions with human-like causality to solve open-domain problems. On the other hand, the evaluator agent scrutinizes if a solution is deducible from a non-causal perspective and if it still holds when challenged by a counterfactual candidate. According to the extensive and comprehensive evaluations on a variety of knowledge reasoning tasks (e.g., science question answering and commonsense reasoning), our framework outperforms all compared state-of-the-art approaches by large margins.

Introduction

The current advancements in large language models (LLMs) usher in a new era. Through the assimilation of global knowledge and integration of human feedback, the most advanced LLMs, equipped with billions of parameters, have the potential to evolve into a colossal knowledge warehouse. However, despite these advancements, the capability of reasoning, which is a fundamental aspect of intelligence, remains a longstanding challenge for even the most cutting-edge LLMs while it depends strongly on task planning, sub-task execution, and the utilization of prior results. The core problem is that the LLM reasoning process is usually fragile, prone to disruption by incomprehension of queries, factualness errors, and inference errors (Zhao et¬†al. 2023 ) , turning out to perform poorly in reasoning.

Recent approaches elicit reasoning in LLMs by mimicking human step-wise reasoning process (Wei et¬†al. 2022b ) from multiple given demonstrations or borrowing external knowledge from a handcrafted database (Peng et¬†al. 2023 ) . Alternatively, others primarily allow LLMs to autonomously plan and solve reasoning problems (Wang et¬†al. 2023a ; Press et¬†al. 2022 ) or to create an extensive ensemble of reasoning chains (Wang et¬†al. 2023b ; Fu et¬†al. 2022 ) . These paradigms exhibit several shortcomings: 1) building and searching from a knowledge base may not be efficient in the LLM era. 2) Some may necessitate the extensive sampling of reasoning chains to derive the best result, which is inefficient. 3) High-quality reasoning chains may not stand out from majority voting as they may not emerge in the decoding probability space. 4) The dependence between reasoning steps may not necessarily be linear (each step relies solely on the outcome of the previous step) and could vary with the problems. 5) They cannot alleviate inference errors in intermediate steps.

Several approaches are proposed to address the above issues. For Shortcoming 3), Fu et al. propose a complexity-based mechanism to screen out low-quality reasoning chains (Fu et¬†al. 2022 ) . For Shortcoming 4), some researchers are endeavoring to augment the non-linearity of the reasoning structure, evolving chain-of-thought reasoning towards more intricate structures, such as tree-of-thought (Yao et¬†al. 2023 ) or graph-of-thought (Yao, Li, and Zhao 2023 ) . However, most of the existing approaches underperform in the knowledge reasoning task without explicitly addressing Shortcomings 3) and 5).

As demonstrated in (Du et¬†al. 2023 ; Talebirad and Nadiri 2023 ) , multi-agent collaboration enhances the robustness of task completion, such as the mixture of experts (MoE) (OpenAI 2023 ) , programming-based interaction (Hong et¬†al. 2023 ) , and debate (Du et¬†al. 2023 ) , where multiple agents can play different or the same roles. However, it should be noted that the quality of outcomes hinges critically on the cooperation mode. For example, a majority consistency may fall short when intelligent agents in a group harbor common biases. Drawing on this understanding, we seek causal consistency within a multi-agent system where intelligent agents with different roles and weights engage in a reasoning-and-consensus process to accommodate potential errors.

Hence, we suggest achieving robust and effective knowledge-based reasoning through a causal-consistency reasoning framework, named CaCo-CoT, without extra knowledge acquisition and large-scale reasoning chain sampling. As shown in Fig. 1 , this framework introduces multiple agents in a hierarchy, including a group of reasoners and an evaluator . The final answer is accepted until they achieve at least one type of consensus.

We prompt each reasoner with procedural guidance. They are responsible for executing prerequisite steps sequentially, i.e., terms explanation , subquestion decomposition , rationale before arriving at an answer . Subsequently, a fair evaluator is introduced to evaluate causal consistency within one or more top solutions from an non-causal direction and challenge these solutions with a counterfactual answer. Once a consensus between two types of agents is formed, we take the consensual answer as the final decision. Otherwise, we execute these procedures recursively until one is achieved.

As we notice that contemporary LLMs have been trained on massive data with general knowledge and tuned for eliminating bias, we introduce two steps prior to the traditional chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning for the reasoner to reduce factual and inferential errors: 1) explaining necessary concepts or principles and 2) breaking down the problem into a list of subquestions. The first step splits off the preliminary knowledge explanation as an independent step. It can be fundamental and tractable for LLMs to recall factual contextual knowledge. Furthermore, with accurate pertinent knowledge as a premise, this step helps alleviate erroneous knowledge inference in the later steps. 3) Finally, we prompt the reasoner to generate its entire rationale and answer based on the question breakdown and knowledge elaboration. The reasoning process of the reasoner can be considered causal since it corresponds to how humans reason based on their knowledge.

To ensure reasoning faithfulness of the solutions by inspecting causal consistency, an evaluator is introduced. Initially, the evaluator is prompted to examine the statements in the solution step by step while considering the whole reasoning structure, which is in a non-causal direction. For further causal consistency assessment, the evaluator then moves on to apply a counterfactual answer to the original question and look for any contradiction therein. Finally, it turns out a modified answer after its reconsideration. The group of agents cooperates for a consensual answer if the most frequent solution is accepted by the evaluator .

In the tasks of science question answering and commonsense reasoning, we demonstrate that our causal-consistency framework outperforms the existing approaches through a series of experiments and comparisons.

Related Work

Advancement in Large Language Models

When scaling up Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs), emergent abilities (e.g., such as arithmetic reasoning (Chung et¬†al. 2022 ) , chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning (Wei et¬†al. 2022b ) ) can enable LLMs to address a wide range of complex tasks (Wei et¬†al. 2022a ) . However, these emergent abilities are rarely present in smaller PLMs, e.g., BERT (Devlin et¬†al. 2019 ) . However, these exceptional LLMs still have limitations, such as producing hallucinations with factual errors or potential risk responses in certain specific contexts (OpenAI 2023 ; Bang et¬†al. 2023 ) . Essentially, LLMs appear to unconsciously utilize knowledge to solve tasks, yet still lack the ability to control the accuracy of knowledge usage (Zhao et¬†al. 2023 ) . Therefore, subsequent studies focus on enhancing CoT‚Äôs potential to improve reasoning accuracy (Fu et¬†al. 2022 ) , including diverse reasoning paths sampling (Wang et¬†al. 2023b ) , sub-problems divided for sequential solving (Zhou et¬†al. 2023 ) , introducing self-asking and self-answering (Press et¬†al. 2022 ) , and employing multi-agent debates (Du et¬†al. 2023 ) .

Knowledge-based Reasoning

For better improving the timeliness and factuality of the responses from LLMs, several works tried to strengthen, update and discriminate LLMs‚Äô responses by introducing external knowledge. This knowledge can be embedded into dense vector index via a seq2seq language model to solve open domain NLP task (Lewis et¬†al. 2020 ) , or retrieved as document chunks via local similarity with preceding tokens (Borgeaud et¬†al. 2022 ) . Similarly, knowledge can be formatted into logical forms, combined with response in natural language to obtain the final answer (Yu et¬†al. 2022 ) . Additionally, introducing knowledge via reinforcement learning can provide automatic feedback on the responses and iteratively revise the responses to improve their factuality (Peng et¬†al. 2023 ) .

Causality in LLMs

The causal abilities of LLMs are a highly debated issue (Willig et¬†al. 2023 ) , but it is undeniable that LLMs have made significant improvements on multiple causal benchmarks (Kƒ±cƒ±man et¬†al. 2023 ) . Additionally, traditional causal methods heavily rely on potential causal mechanisms and prior domain knowledge within a system that cannot be explicitly demonstrated (Stolfo et¬†al. 2022 ) . Furthermore, causal knowledge in traditional causal methods is difficult to transfer to other domains, limiting the practicality of causal methods. In contrast, LLMs utilize background knowledge about the real world as prior information, enabling them to comprehend and formalize diverse causal scenarios and use this knowledge to make accurate reasoning about unseen events (Chen et¬†al. 2023 ) . Specifically, the emergence capability of LLMs can be leveraged to perform causal discovery using the internal knowledge of the model. (Long et¬†al. 2023 ) generated simple causal structures for a limited set of variables mentioned in the text using LLMs, but this relied on the connectors between variables in the text. Therefore, (Ban et¬†al. 2023 ) further integrated the generated causal knowledge as input, enabling LLMs to discover more faithful and novel causal relationships from data, and integrated LLMs as a causal knowledge querying tool into classical models, bringing significant performance improvement.

Causal-consistency in Knowledge-based Reasoning

This paper focuses on addressing knowledge-based reasoning (KR) problems described in natural language via Large Language Models (LLMs). These problems require background knowledge that usually does not appear in the question, as a premise for reasoning, such as science question answering and commonsense reasoning. LLMs have learned to conduct knowledge inferring, that is to employ acquired knowledge to derive unknown knowledge, including necessary intermediate and final results. We focus on LLMs with over 100B parameters (Ouyang et¬†al. 2022 ; Bai et¬†al. 2022 ) as they are more likely to have obtained the necessary background knowledge.

The core idea of the causal consistency framework is to solve a KR problem through a decision-making process where multiple agents independently play the role of either a reasoner or an evaluator . Each agent is implemented by prompting an LLM with in-context learning capability. Towards a Faithful Reasoner

In the first stage, a group of independent reasoner agents depends on a procedure to reason and arrive at a potential answer, i.e., procedural reasoning. The procedure that an agent learns from a given prompt template involves a sequence of { concept explanation , subquestion decomposition , rationale generation , answer synthesization }. By following the procedure, an reasoner agent learns to reason for a specific question in a causal style since it mimics human causality. We elaborate procedural reasoning of reasoner in details:

Step 1: Concept explanation is to clarify any technical terms, concepts, principles, etc. This step demands an LLM to recall necessary conceptual knowledge from its parameter space, i.e., transforming the parameterized form into natural language form. It ensures each reasoner agent has an unbiased understanding of the terms used in the question, aiming to reduce the possibility of misinterpretation or ambiguity in the knowledge inference.

Step 2: Subquestion decomposition breaks down the main question into manageable single-step subquestions and solves them. These subquestions are usually connected logically to navigate reasoning toward the final answer. The agent will solve the subquestions one by one, leveraging the knowledge from the concept explanation . This step-by-step approach allows the agent to simplify complex problems and ensures a comprehensive exploration of the issue at hand. The individual answers to the subquestions are then stored for integration in the final answer.

Step 3: The rationale generation step is to reason for the answer with contexts from steps 1 and 2. Each agent applies logic, principle, and any available knowledge to arrive at the answer to the original question,

Step 4: Answer synthesization is the concluding phase of the reasoning process. In this step, evidence, findings, and conclusions from previous steps are integrated to formulate a comprehensive response. The synthesized answer should provide a clear and definitive conclusion. For subsequent use, we separate the answer (typically a chosen option) from each solution which includes all reasoning steps.

Boost the Evaluator via Non-causal Evaluation and Counterfactual Reasoning

For reasoners , the auto-regressive generation ( Radford et¬†al. ; Radford et¬†al. 2018 ) of a solution is witnessed, which means that the outcome at each step t ùë° t heavily relies on the preceding steps from 1 1 1 to t ‚àí 1 ùë° 1 t-1 . Knowledge reasoning problems can be considered causal because one may require pertinent background knowledge (Step 1) (cause) to infer the next three steps‚Äô results (effects). For instance, decomposing subquestions (Step 2) necessitates a clear comprehension of the concepts in the question (Step 1) to acknowledge their interdependencies, indicated as Step 1 ‚Üí ‚Üí \rightarrow Step 2.

Prior work shows that LLMs are capable of recognizing both factual or inferential mistakes (Li et¬†al. 2023 ) , so we introduce an evaluator agent that aims to assess the faithfulness of potential solutions gathered from reasoners during the process. To further reduce factualness and inference errors, the task for the evaluator agent is to examine causal consistency within the solution as working from a non-causal perspective and a counterfactual perspective. The specific functions of the evaluator are described as follows:

Step 1: Non-causal Evaluation .

To evaluate the factuality of knowledge and inferential soundness, the evaluator agent works from a non-causal direction. In this direction, all the intermediate steps of a solution are provided to the evaluator agent as its context. The agent subsequently inspects each step carefully while taking the solution into consideration, i.e., (Step 1, Step 2, Step 3, Step 4) ‚Üí ‚Üí \rightarrow Eval (Step 1).

This type of evaluation can be considered non-causal in this paper since the solution is evaluated after it has been completed and read through, which is not operated in the direction of the causal reasoning process. It provides an opportunity to identify any emergent properties or macro-level patterns (such as dependency relationship in the rationale) that might be missed when analyzing the system at a micro-level (reasoning step-by-step to the answer without considering where it leads to).

Step 2: Counterfactual Evaluation . To deeply detect inference and factualness fallacies, the evaluator is prompted to evaluate if the original solution still holds true when scrutinized from a different direction.

Specifically, the counterfactual answer is a candidate answer distinct from the one in the given solution. This reconsideration course is implemented by applying a counterfactual answer to the original question and searching for any contradiction.

Step 3: Reconsideration and Answer Synthesis . In the previous two steps, the evaluator assesses the causal consistency of a given solution through non-causal evaluation and counterfactual analysis. If any causal inconsistency is detected, the agent evaluates the relative merits of the counterfactual answer against the initial response. Otherwise, further reasoning may not be required and the agent can confidently output the original answer.

Experiments

To demonstrate the robustness and effectiveness of our proposed method, we have conducted a series of experiments comparing several existing methods on a range of datasets.

ScienceQA (Lu et¬†al. 2022 ) is a multimodal multiple choice dataset used to develop a faithful model clearly uncovering the multi-step reasoning process. It consists of approximately 21k questions with rich domain diversity, annotated with image and text context. It is split into train/validation/test sets with a ratio of 60:20:20. In our experiment, we only employ the test set samples described in the text, with a total of 2224. We report the accuracy of our method on the set mentioned above.

Com2Sense (Singh et¬†al. 2021 ) is a commonsense reasoning dataset consisting of 4k sentence pairs annotated with true or false labels. It aims to systematically evaluate a model‚Äôs capability of commonsense knowledge understanding and reasoning. In our experiment, we compare existing methods with ours on the development set, using accuracy.

BoolQ (Clark et¬†al. 2019 ) is a reading comprehension dataset labeled with yes/no questions. It consists of 16k questions paired with a paragraph from Wikipedia annotated as containing the answer. In detail, it is split into a 3.2k dev set, a 3.2k test set, and a 9.4k train set. We use the dev set to evaluate the methods with accuracy, in a closed-book setting.

Commonsense Reasoning

Commonsense reasoning aims to answer questions that require reasoning over general background knowledge. Commonsense reasoning capability is essential for AI deployed in the real world. Strong commonsense reasoning capabilities restrict AI from functioning within reasonable and safe boundaries. Specifically, we employ the same experiment setting as Science Question Answering.
As shown in Table 1 , our method outperforms the existing methods in both Com2Sense and BoolQ. As we observed, CoT-based approaches (Wei et¬†al. 2022b ; Wang et¬†al. 2023b ) usually fall short of correctly employing relevant background knowledge implied by the question, because they often embark on knowledge inference before having a clear definition of the knowledge in the context of the given question. Step-by-step reasoning may proceed in a ‚Äùplan-as-you-go‚Äù manner (i.e., without a clear plan to arrive at the answer), which may be misleading when facing multi-hop reasoning problems. Our approach prompts the reasoner agents to explicitly extract the knowledge and decompose the question into a group of subquestions before reasoning, which is a low-risk and effective way to perform knowledge inference. Moreover, the solution will be inspected by our evaluator agent to ensure causal consistency, including non-causal inspection and counterfactual reasoning for another potential answer. Notably, our approach showcases its powerful reasoning capability. There is more than 3% accuracy improvement for the zero-shot setting and 2.6% for the one-shot setting on Com2sense in GPT-3.5-turbo. Additionally, around 3.5% accuracy improvement on BoolQ and 2.4% on Com2sense in Claude can be witnessed, only through learning to follow the reasoning and evaluating procedure. Overall, our CaCo-CoT approach mostly outperforms the state-of-the-art counterparts on the Com2sense and BoolQ datasets.

Furthermore, the experiments of CaCo-CoT R3&E0 and CaCo-CoT R3&E1 in both settings discover that even if three independent reasoners (CaCo-CoT R3&E0 ) produce erroneous inference and incorrect answers, the evaluators consistently recover them to causal consistent ones, with average accuracy lifted by over 1.2%. Notably, our implementation of CaCo-CoT R3&E0 dramatically boosts the accuracy in harder questions of grade 7-12 only taking a grade-2 demonstration, while previous approaches reach the limit of 83.85%.

Limitations and Conclusion

Concerning the limitations of our approach, when the collection of agents is sharing a foundation LLM, they harbor shared biases inevitably. This is embodied when they confidently reason out an erroneous outcome. Also, individual reasoners occasionally exhibit error accumulation, wherein errors in the prerequisite steps cause more errors in subsequent steps. Especially, reasoners can adapt to the golden answer when the background knowledge (golden evidence) is given. In addition, our method is unable to effectively screen out the most faithful solution under extreme circumstances, such as continuous inconsistencies.

In conclusion, in this paper, we address the prevalent issue of factualness and inference fallacies intrinsic in contemporary LLMs via a multi-agent cooperation framework, named CaCo-CoT, for knowledge-based reasoning. This framework comprises of two types of agents - the reasoner and the evaluator , working together in a reasoning-and-consensus paradigm to ensure causal consistency. Our experiments, carried out on a wide array of knowledge reasoning tasks, indicate that our method allows LLM-based agents to yield high-quality responses, assess the reasoning process fairly, and finally correct the outcomes. By comparing with the existing method, our proposed method demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the most of benchmarks.

Error Analysis of CaCo-CoT

Bias in LLMs

There is an erroneous case for a dictionary guide word question on ScienceQA. In this case, the two reasoners in CaCo-CoT assert a false argument, i.e., the word ‚Äôguess‚Äô comes after ‚Äôgarage‚Äô and before ‚Äôgoose‚Äô alphabetically . Worse still, the evaluator consents to this fallacy and also incorrectly considers the answer (B), i.e., if ‚Äùglow‚Äù is the word on the dictionary page, it would contradict the alphabetical order between ‚Äùgarage‚Äù and ‚Äùgoose‚Äù. . Finally, these agents makes a consensus on the answer (A) guess, while the CoT-based approaches can have a 50% probability of answering with a correct answer.

The limitations of contemporary LLMs in handling dictionary guide word questions are related to the underlying word embedding technique used in these models. It focuses more on capturing semantic relationships among words based on their contextual usage patterns in the training data instead of word structure information found in dictionaries. Eventually, words with similar meanings or usage contexts are represented by high-dimensional vectors that are closer together in this embedding space. As a result, LLMs may struggle to provide concise answers for this type of query. This may be one of the intrinsic biases within the LLM which we cannot fully address in this paper.

It is important to note that our CaCo-CoT does not entirely eliminate unfaithful reasoning.
