Abstract

This paper explores the application of Swarm-Structured Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) to establish medical necessity, a process that involves a systematic review of patient-specific medical structured and unstructured data against clinical guidelines. We addressed this complex task by decomposing it into smaller, more manageable sub-tasks. Each sub-task is handled by a specialized AI agent. We conduct a systematic study of the impact of various prompting strategies on these agents and benchmark different Large Language Models (LLMs) to determine their accuracy in completing these tasks. Additionally, we investigate how these agents can provide explainability, thereby enhancing trust and transparency within the system.

1 Introduction

In US healthcare, management of administrative workflows represents a pivotal yet formidable challenge. Physicians, nurses, and administrative personnel frequently allocate a substantial portion of their working hours to these procedural tasks, detracting from their primary focus on patient care. Establishing or verifying medical necessity for Prior Authorization is a process that forms a part of several administrative tasks of US Healthcare, necessitates considerable time and resource investment, impacting the overall efficiency of healthcare delivery.

Prior authorization (PA) is a healthcare management process used by insurance entities to determine whether a proposed treatment or service, including but not limited to medications, imaging, and procedures, is covered under a patientâ€™s plan before it is approved to be carried out. Madhusoodanan etÂ al. ( 2023 ) . Evaluating a PA application involves assessing medical necessity of patient-specific health records against prevailing coverage guidelines. A major part of these coverage guidelines are clinical guidelines which are systematically developed statements designed to help practitioners make decisions about appropriate health care for specific clinical circumstances. Insurance companies review these clinical guidelines to to justify medical necessity of a procedure Chambers etÂ al. ( 2016 ) . A majority of PAs filed today are created manually by healthcare professionals extensively collecting evidences that can justify these medical necessity guidelines. In this paper we explore the application of multi-agent systems in automating the process of determining medical necessity by comparing clinical guidelines with the Electronic Health Records (EHR) of a patient.

The introduction of Large Language Models (LLMs) has catalyzed a transformative shift in the capabilities of artificial intelligence, enabling the resolution of complex challenges previously inaccessible to conventional AI methods. LLMs excel in interpreting and synthesizing large volumes of unstructured data, enhancing tasks such as natural language understanding, sentiment analysis, and automated content creation. Building on this foundation, Swarm Intelligence architecture , which employs a collective of AI-powered agents, represents an even further advancement Li and Clerc ( 2019 ) . This approach decomposes a singular complex task into multiple, manageable sub-tasks and distributes them across multiple agents, each specialized through training for a sub-task. Following this methodology essentially infuses a microservice architecture into the traditional monolithic AI framework, enabling more modular, scalable, and robust AI systems. By integrating the depth and adaptability of LLMs with the collaborative and dynamic nature of Swarm Intelligence architecture, AI systems can achieve unprecedented levels of performance and versatility across various complex problems.

In this paper, we investigate the application of multi-agent systems for determining medical necessity. We break down the entire process into several sub-tasks, each handled by a different Large Language Model (LLM) agent. We explore a variety of prompting strategies for each agent and examine the effects of the specific LLM used on these agents, particularly focusing on the trade-off between accuracy and latency.

Motivated by this observation, we propose a two step solution for our problem statement. First we determine the judgement of each of the leaf node checklist items. Subsequently, we propagate the solution for parent nodes bottom-up using Depth First Search.

4 Experiments and Results

We have conducted preliminary tests for the Leaf-Node Judgment Prediction task on a limited set of examples, and a broader evaluation on a more extensive dataset is currently underway. Updates to the paper will follow upon completion of the study.

For the Bottom-Up Judgment Propagation task, we have prepared approximately 300 sub-checklists, where each sub-checklist includes a parent node and its associated child nodes. To evaluate our Propagator Agent , we intentionally apply the judgments from the Leaf-Node Judgment Prediction task to each leaf node in the checklist. This method of synthetic assignment is advantageous as it introduces a range of less likely or extraordinary judgment combinations, thereby challenging our Propagator Agent to maximize its robustness.

Our Propagator Agent is an LLM-powered Agent, which takes up a parent node and its corresponding leaf nodes (along with their judgments and confidence scores) to obtain the judgment and confidence score of the parent node. Due to proprietary constraints, the specific prompt utilized for this agent is not disclosed; however, we evaluate the performance of the Propagator Agent across various dimensions. 4.1 Effect of Prompting Strategy

We performed two sets of experiments. The first involved providing In-Context Learning (ICL) examples Min etÂ al. ( 2022 ) and measuring accuracy, as illustrated in Figure 3 . Larger models such as GPT-4 and Opus yielded strong results, whereas smaller models like Mixtral-8X7B and GPT-3.5 exhibited suboptimal performance when relying solely on ICL prompts. However, in the second experiment, when supplemented with Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting Wei etÂ al. ( 2022 ) , the performance of these smaller models markedly improved, demonstrating how the step-by-step reasoning process aids in decomposing the complex task of propagation into manageable segments.

However, the use of Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting substantially increases response times for larger models due to its generation of an increased number of tokens compared to ICL-only prompting. In contrast, the enhancements in performance observed with GPT-3.5 are achieved without a marked increase in latency, particularly when compared to larger models such as Opus and GPT-4 under similar conditions.

4.2 Effect of LLM Choice

We conducted an evaluation of the Propagator Agent utilizing various LLMs, with a particular emphasis on metrics such as accuracy and latency. Opus and GPT-4 emerged as the top performers, achieving approximately 94% accuracy when CoT prompting was combined with ICL examples, as illustrated in Figure 3 .

GPT-3.5 is ranked second in terms of accuracy but presents significant benefits in reduced latency compared to GPT-4 and Opus, as depicted in Figure 4 . Additionally, the operational costs associated with GPT-3.5 are substantially lower. Although selecting the optimal model involves a trade-off, GPT-3.5 stands out as the preferred option when considering a balance among cost, latency, and accuracy. Nonetheless, for scenarios where maximum accuracy is crucial, the larger models such as GPT-4 and Opus are more appropriate.

5 Conclusion

We have demonstrated that decomposing a large, complex problem into a series of smaller, specialized tasks handled by different agents can help us automate even sophisticated tasks that until now was extremely difficult. This approach also enables us to illustrate the transition from a monolithic AI solution ( â„³ â„³ \mathcal{M} ) to a more micro-service architecture driven solution ( â„³ r subscript â„³ ğ‘Ÿ \mathcal{M}_{r} , â„³ j subscript â„³ ğ‘— \mathcal{M}_{j} and â„³ p subscript â„³ ğ‘ \mathcal{M}_{p} ). At present, our methodology utilizes a constrained workflow; however, there is potential to develop a system incorporating loosely coupled agents. These agents would be more dynamic and agile, enhancing their capacity for problem-solving.

A fully developed implementation of this approach would essentially adopt an organizational-like structure, where the entire architecture is composed of several pods. Each pod contains worker agents, each specialized in different facets of the given problem, along with checker agents that reassess and validates worker agentâ€™s output, prompting a rerun if the outputs appear to be incorrect. Overseeing these pods would be a super-orchestrator agent, analogous to a CEO at the organizational level, who coordinates and directs the activities across the architecture. This architectural design significantly addresses the issue of hallucinations, a common challenge in existing LLMs.

Moreover, given our workâ€™s focus on the healthcare industry, there is a substantial emphasis on the explainability of the outputs generated by these LLM agents. The logs produced through Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting provide valuable insights into the decision-making processes of these agents, addressing, to some extent, the challenges of transparency associated with employing AI models in healthcare workflows.
