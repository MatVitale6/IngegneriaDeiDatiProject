Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown remarkable capabilities in general natural language processing tasks but often fall short in complex reasoning tasks. Recent studies have explored human-like problem-solving strategies, such as self-correct , to push further the boundary of single-model reasoning ability. In this work, we let a single model â€œstep outside the boxâ€ by engaging multiple models to correct each other. We introduce a multi-agent collaboration strategy that emulates the academic peer review process. Each agent independently constructs its own solution, provides reviews on the solutions of others, and assigns confidence levels to its reviews. Upon receiving peer reviews, agents revise their initial solutions. Extensive experiments on three different types of reasoning tasks show that our collaboration approach delivers superior accuracy across all ten datasets compared to existing methods. Further study underscores the effectiveness of integrating confidence in reviews, demonstrates the superiority of feedback exchange over mere solution sharing, and highlights the role of capability and diversity in fostering successful collaboration. Code is available at https://github.com/HITsz-TMG/Multi-agent-peer-review .

Amidst this background, inspired by the typical human learning strategy of â€œself-reflectionâ€, the idea of â€œself-correctionâ€ has gained significant research interest, where an LLM automatically reflects on its response and refines based solely on its inherent capabilities Madaan etÂ al. This iterative cycle of creation, review, and revision is akin to the collaborative efforts seen in human learning and problem-solving.

We conduct extensive experiments across three types of reasoning tasks: mathematical reasoning, commonsense reasoning, and symbolic reasoning. The results illustrate that our collaboration method achieves superior performance across all ten datasets compared to previous strong single-agent and multi-agent baselines. 2 Related Work

With language models scaling up, the emergent abilities have showcased remarkable results across a variety of domains and tasks, challenging our understanding of learning and cognition Bubeck etÂ al. ( 2023 ); OpenAI ( 2023 ) . Among these rising capabilities, complex reasoning draws significant interest for its central role in building autonomous language agents and the next-generation computation platform Xi etÂ al. ( 2023 ); Fu etÂ al. ( 2023 ) .

To push further the boundary of single-agent reasoning , researchers have adopted strategies akin to teaching kids to reason. For example, the chain-of-thought prompting Wei etÂ al. ( 2022b ) employs rationales to steer LLMs through a multi-step reasoning process; the self-consistency method Wang etÂ al. ( 2023b ) selects the most likely answer from multiple reasoning paths by majority voting; the plan-and-solve prompting Wang etÂ al. ( 2023a ) and the least-to-most prompting Zhou etÂ al. ( 2023 ) both apply a divide-and-conquer strategy: break down a complex problem into a series of simpler sub-problems and then solve them in sequence.

Amidst this backdrop, a prevailing strategy to address reasoning errors is self-correction , mirroring a typical human learning strategy of self-reflection Pan etÂ al. ( 2023 ) . This approach involves an LLM attempting to refine its prior response based solely on its inherent capabilities, without the crutch of external feedback. Contrary to the optimism about self-correction Madaan etÂ al. ( 2023 ); Shinn etÂ al. ( 2023 ); Kim etÂ al. ( 2023 ) , Huang etÂ al. ( 2023 ) find that an LLM struggles to correct its response without external feedback. Liang etÂ al. ( 2023 ) also conclude that, once the LLM has formulated initial solutions, it cannot generate novel thoughts through self-reflection. In this work, we let a single LLM â€œjump outside the boxâ€ and change its viewpoint from a participant to a critical observer by enabling multiple LLMs to correct each other.

Multi-agent collaboration for task solving is still in its preliminary stages, presenting vast opportunities for exploration. This approach aligns with the Society of Mind (SoM) theory Minsky ( 1988 ) , which suggests that intelligence arises from the interaction of computational modules, achieving collective goals beyond the capabilities of individual modules. CAMEL Li etÂ al. ( 2023a ) proposes a cooperative agent framework known as role-playing, enabling agents to cooperate on complex tasks. Such role-playing collaboration has been extended to software development Qian etÂ al. ( 2023 ) and text evaluation Chan etÂ al. ( 2023 ); Li etÂ al. ( 2023b ) . Liang etÂ al. ( 2023 ) and Du etÂ al. ( 2023 ) have delved into multi-agent debates for translation and arithmetic problems. Corex Sun etÂ al. ( 2023 ) has explored three collaboration paradigms in reasoning â€“ debate, code review and retrieve. Through these multi-agent simulations, Zhang etÂ al. ( 2023 ) observe human-like social behaviors typical of human collaboration. To this end, we propose a human-mimicking multi-agent collaboration process, wherein multiple agents engage in peer discussions and provide feedback to one another.

The final prediction is determined through a majority vote among the n ğ‘› n participating agents. This approach ensures the impartiality and balance of our peer review process.

4.4 Results

Main results. Table 2 shows the accuracy comparison on the mathematical reasoning datasets. Our peer review strategy surpasses all single-agent and multi-agent baselines across all datasets, especially significant in more challenging benchmarks (+1.40% on GSM8K, +3.80% on SVAMP, +2.75% on AQuA over the prior state-of-the-art).

Table 3 and Table 4 show the results on commonsense reasoning and symbolic reasoning datasets, respectively. Our collaboration method consistently outperforms all prior baselines across all datasets. The effectiveness of self-correction notably decreases in non-math reasoning tasks, as seen with a substantial drop from 66.13% to 28.27% in accuracy on Colored Objects. However, when we introduce feedback from other agents, there is a significant enhancement in performance, rising from 66.13% to 73.20%. This demonstrates the advantage of integrating external feedback over relying solely on internal feedback mechanisms.

Ablation study. For the ablation of confidence in reviews, we alter the prompt of Stage 2 described in Section 3 . Specifically, we remove the prompt of eliciting confidence, simplifying it to: â€œPlease examine this agentâ€™s reasoning process step by step and offer feedback on its reasoningâ€ . Table 2 shows the effectiveness of expressing uncertainty and integrating confidence in feedback on mathematical reasoning datasets. An example of confidence ablation is shown in Appendix B . As stated in the prompt of Stage 3, the updated answer uses other agentsâ€™ solutions and feedback as additional information. In the absence of confidence scores, the updated answer tends to be misled by ineffective feedback and remains incorrect. In contrast, the integration of confidence score enables the model to selectively aggregate valuable information. By focusing more on solutions with higher confidence levels rather than on less reliable feedback, the confidence scores guide the model towards the correct answer.

For commonsense reasoning in Table 3 and symbolic reasoning in Table 4 , incorporating confidence can result in unchanged or even reduced performance. This decrease may stem from the tendency of LLMs to exhibit overconfidence and significant miscalibration when verbalizing their confidence in non-math datasets Xiong etÂ al. ( 2023b ) . We will further discuss the relationship between the level of confidence and the correctness of feedback in Section 4.5 .

For the ablation of solutions , we leave out the the dialogue history of Stage 2 during the answer revision process in Stage 3, so that the peer solutions cannot be seen. We also change the prompt of Stage 3 into â€œUsing other agentsâ€™ feedbacks as additional information, can you provide your answer to the problem? â€ . From Table 2 , 3 and 4 , omitting solutions resulted in decreased performance in 9 out of 10 datasets, indicating that solutions contribute valuable complementary information to the feedback received. In addition, Ours (w/o solution) , which involves exchanging only peer feedback, outperforms Multi-agent Debate , which exchanges only peer solutions, in 8 out of 10 datasets. This demonstrates that peer feedback is more effective than peer solutions in multi-agent collaboration.

5 Conclusion

In this work, we let a single model think outside the box by engaging multiple models to collaborate on complex reasoning. We introduce a multi-agent collaboration strategy that emulates the academic peer review process, following an iterative cycle of creation, review, and revision. Extensive experiments show that our approach delivers superior accuracy across all ten datasets and three tasks compared to existing methods. Further analysis shows the effectiveness of confidence integration and demonstrates the superiority of feedback exchange over mere solution sharing.

Limitations

Despite the effectiveness of multi-agent methods, it is essential to consider the associated costs, particularly in terms of token consumption. The balance between effectiveness and efficiency emerges as a critical area for exploration. In addition, the underlying factors for LLM collaboration are more than just capability and answer diversity. The factors also include role and tool specification of agents. Further research may focus on identifying which types of agents can collaborate more effectively together, thereby providing deeper insights into the optimization of multi-agent systems in practical applications.

Appendix A Implementation Details

We use the gpt-3.5-turbo-0613 OpenAI API for all experiments, with all parameters in default setting. We fix the agent number as 3 and the round number of review cycle as 1. We also apply gpt-3.5-turbo-16k-0613 to explore scenarios with a greater number of agents and additional review rounds in Section 4.5 . Specifically, we utilize the 16k version for experiments when the total count of agents is set to 5. We also use the 16k version for an increased number of review cycles. The review is conducted over four rounds, with accuracy being calculated after each round, so the result at the first round in Figure 4 may differ from the reported result in Table 2 .

Our implementation of the self-correct prompt draws from Huang etÂ al. ( 2023 ) . For the implementation of multi-agent debate Du etÂ al. ( 2023 ) , we utilize the official Github repository 1 1 1 The code for Du etÂ al. ( 2023 ) is at https://github.com/composable-models/llm_multiagent_debate . This implementation adheres to the default configuration of three agents and two debate rounds.

For evaluation metric, we follow Kojima etÂ al. ( 2022 ) and report the accuracy of all methods across datasets. Due to the rate limits and a restricted budget, our experiments use a maximum of 500 samples, randomly selected from each dataset.

Appendix D Impact of Diverse Role Prompts

We conduct experiments on GSM8K and SVAMP. The following five roles are automatically generated through SPP Wang etÂ al. ( 2023c ) :

AI Assistant. You are an advanced AI capable of performing complex calculations, logical reasoning, and providing efficient solutions to mathematical problems.

Math Teacher. You are an experienced math teacher with a deep understanding of mathematical concepts, methods, and pedagogy. You can offer insights into problem-solving strategies and educational approaches to mathematics.

Mathematical Scientist. You are a scientist who specializes in advanced mathematical theories and research. You can contribute deep theoretical knowledge and expertise in cutting-edge mathematical areas, such as topology, abstract algebra, or number theory.

Engineer. You are a professional engineer with practical experience in applying mathematical concepts to solve real-world problems. You can offer a practical viewpoint on how mathematics is used in designing, analyzing, and improving systems and structures.

Computer Scientist. You are an expert in computer science, well-versed in algorithms, computational theory, and programming. You can assist in solving problems that involve algorithmic thinking, computational complexity, or numerical methods.

We study whether it is necessary to design diverse role prompts for multi-agent collaboration. Table 7 shows the experiment results. In the single role setting, all agents are AI assistants. In the diverse role setting, for every question, agent roles are randomly selected from the above 5 roles. The results illustrate that our method with the same role prompt underperforms that with diverse role prompt, highlighting the cruciality of diverse role specification in multi-agent collaboration.
